Best Orbital Sander Sandpaper for Auto Body

Auto body work demands precision. The wrong grit or a cheap disc can leave scratches that show through paint, or worse, burn through primer. Here are the best 5" sanding discs for automotive paint prep, primer sanding, and clear coat finishing.

Best Overall for Auto Body

Top Pick — Auto Body

Mirka Abranet Sanding Discs

Mirka

Mirka Abranet is the disc of choice in professional auto body shops. The mesh construction provides dust extraction across the entire surface, which is critical when sanding primer and filler — dust buildup causes scratches. Available from P80 all the way to P1000, Abranet covers every stage of auto body prep. The consistent scratch pattern means predictable results when you spray.

Pros

  • Professional-grade dust extraction
  • Consistent scratch pattern for paint adhesion
  • Available up to P1000 for clear coat work
  • Works with any vacuum-equipped DA sander
  • Trusted by professional painters

Cons

  • Most expensive option
  • Requires vacuum extraction for best results
  • Overkill for rough body work

Best for Paint Stripping & Rust Removal

Top Pick — Stripping

3M Cubitron II Hookit Discs (36–80 Grit)

3M

When you need to strip paint down to bare metal or remove surface rust, Cubitron II's ceramic grain is hard to beat. The precision-shaped triangles cut aggressively without loading up, and they run cooler than standard discs — important when working on metal panels where heat can cause warping. The 36 and 60 grit options are particularly effective for stripping.

Pros

  • Aggressive cutting on paint and rust
  • Runs cool — less risk of panel warping
  • Long disc life even on metal
  • Self-sharpening grain

Cons

  • Expensive for rough stripping work
  • Too aggressive for primer sanding

Best Budget for Auto Body Prep

Budget Pick — Auto

Diablo SandNet Discs

Diablo (Freud)

For DIY auto body work where you don't want to spend Mirka money, Diablo SandNet is a solid alternative. The mesh design provides good dust extraction (important for primer sanding), and the ceramic alumina grain handles body filler and primer well. Available up to 320 grit, which covers most prep work before you switch to wet sanding.

Pros

  • Good dust extraction at a lower price
  • Handles body filler and primer well
  • No hole-pattern matching
  • Available at Home Depot

Cons

  • Max 320 grit — need other discs for fine work
  • Not as consistent as Mirka for final prep

Auto Body Sanding Stages

Stage Grit Purpose Method
Paint stripping 36–80 Remove old paint down to bare metal DA sander, dry
Body filler shaping 80–120 Shape and level Bondo or filler DA sander or block, dry
Filler finishing 180–220 Smooth filler before primer DA sander, dry
Primer sanding 320–400 Smooth primer, check for imperfections DA sander or block, dry or wet
Pre-paint prep 500–600 Final surface prep before base coat Wet sand by hand or DA
Clear coat leveling 800–1500 Remove orange peel, level clear Wet sand, then compound
Final polish 2000–3000 Pre-polish scratch removal Wet sand, then polish
Important: For grits above 600, wet sanding is strongly recommended. Dry sanding at ultra-fine grits generates heat and can damage clear coat. Use a spray bottle to keep the surface wet, and let the disc do the work — minimal pressure.
DA vs. block sanding: A random orbital (DA) sander is great for large flat panels and general prep. But for final primer sanding and checking for waves, hand-blocking with a sanding block gives you better feel for surface imperfections. Many pros use both.
